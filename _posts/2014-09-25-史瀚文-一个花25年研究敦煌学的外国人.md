【编者注】本文是史瀚文博士（NeilSchmid）在《大家》的专栏开篇。史瀚文博士是美国乔治城大学学士，巴黎高等研究应用学院哲学硕士，后进入宾夕法尼亚大学博士。曾任职于北卡罗来纳州立大学、杜克大学和宾夕法尼亚大学，他主要的学术研究方向是中世纪考古遗址-敦煌莫高窟。

作为一位花了25年研究敦煌学的外国人，史瀚文博士将连续撰文，阐述他对敦煌学的研究心得，以及他对东方文化哲学的理解，对跨文化的比较研究等课题。

近日，《大家》曾发表宋石男先生的《敦煌，吾国学术之伤心史？》一文，关于敦煌学的现状与未来，史瀚文博士的见解，或者能为我们打开一个新视角。

以下为正文。

![](https://t1.qpic.cn/mblogpic/8389272c1f9dca49b868/2000)

敦煌学在西方并不为人所熟知。当家人和朋友们问起我到底在研究些什么，我一般会回答说，那是中国甘肃省丝绸之路上的一个非常特别的考古遗迹。

在西方，几乎没人知道甘肃，或者敦煌，当然更没人听说过莫高窟。然而在这个鲜有人耕耘的未知领域却蕴藏着巨大的财富。无论是提到敦煌，莫高窟，亦或是甘肃省，都会为听众打开无数扇门，这些门通向关于世界历史、中国古代文明，还有，请容我自私地说，关于我的人生和如何成为了一名学者的故事。

然而，要怎样开始描述我们的人生轨迹？如何从一个点走到另一个点，二三十年后我们又会在哪里？早在中学时代，我就有一种很特异的感觉——我知道我想要如何展开我的人生：我希望能从事学术工作。

一个14岁男孩特异的人生假设最终成为他一生的事业，让他去研究遥远的中国西部那一片神奇的遗迹，这一切都是“缘”。

上世纪80年代，当我准备进入大学的时候，我决定去做一些具有挑战性的事情，以获得一种具体的技能。为了让这件事更富于挑战性，我决定选择一个需要从零开始的、我之前对之一无所知的课题，还有什么比学习中文和中国文学更好的选择吗？

在1980年代，对于大多数美国人而言，中国是一个完全陌生的国度，仅仅在70年代初刚与美国建立了外交关系，几年后才又开放了有限的旅游资源，关于中国的一切知识都是那么的匮乏。那个时期离互联网的出现还有十几年，所有的信息都只能从杂志和书籍中获取。美国所有的地方图书馆都只有少得可怜的几本关于中国的介绍。我中学寄宿的学校，虽然已经是全美顶级的学校，也几乎没有关于中国文化的任何介绍，更不要提中文。事实上，直到1989年大学毕业，我拿着中文学士学位回到我的中学时，学校还极其自豪地将我介绍给学校里的第一位中国留学生（我很高兴能看到他后来事业上的成功，在我见到他的十几年后，他建立了“e龙”网）。如今，我的中学已经开设了中文和中国文化的课程，而且还为学生们提供一年在中国交换学习的机会。这在我读书的那个年代是完全不可想象的。

我对中文和中国文化的学习，并不仅仅因为它们极具挑战性，当我还是一个17岁的中学生时，我就获知了中国文化在世界文化史中的地位，并被其深深吸引。青铜器时代曾经盛极一时的世界四大文明（埃及、美索不达米亚、印第斯峡谷和中国），只有中国直到现在还保持着惊人的文化的传承，而其他的几大文明都已失去了这种连续性。将汉字同象形文字、楔形文字，以及尚未破解的哈拉帕文字相比较就可见一斑。无论文化如何变迁，都能以汉字为媒介穿越在历史和当下之间，这种体验，对于一个求知若渴的灵魂来说，简直是不容错过的机会。

于是，在正式进入大学之后，我接受了这项巨大的挑战，从零开始，学习一种完全脱离时尚的语言（那个时期，在美国到处还都是关于日本的文化和介绍……）。

我的中文课每天从早上8点开始，由一位严厉的中国女老师教授。她深谙“羞耻心”在学习中的作用（除了她聪明地利用“羞耻”让我们学习之外，我依旧清晰地记得那时我右手常有的疼痛，那是每周不停地、重复地写上千遍的汉字的结果）。我学业的转折点，发生在大二那年去台湾的时候。我太渴望进入中国开始学习真正的中国文化了。大学毕业以后，我拿到了台湾政治大学中文系的奖学金，再一次回到台湾。在那里，我才真正意义上开始接触到敦煌学和一些敦煌文献，并且非常幸运地师从两位很棒的教授：李丰楙和罗宗涛。通过他们我了解到宾夕法尼亚大学的梅维恒(Victor Mair)教授在敦煌文献和文学方面的研究成果。之后我真的来到宾大攻读我的博士学位。在攻读博士学位期间，我去了巴黎的法国高等实验研究学院同苏远鸣(MichelSoymié)、戴仁(Jean-PierreDrège)两位学者一起从事相关研究。随后又到日本的早稻田大学向佛学家福井文雅学习。最后我再次回到宾大，在宾大的梅维恒教授(VictorMair)和普林斯顿的太史文教授(StephenTeiser)的共同指导下完成了博士论文。

说到这里，我似乎说了太多的名字，但是我坚持认为，他们确实应该被提到。不仅仅是因为他们花费了大量时间培养我，更是因为他们作为学者对于学术研究的献身精神，以及他们在各自领域所达到的成就（唉，学者多是在幕后安静地工作，不为公众所了解）。这些教授都尝试将所有知识传授给我，只是我比较愚笨不能全部掌握。现在回想起来，我觉得比起那些知识本身，我获得更多的是对于严格的学术标准的认同以及对学术的热情，这些都已深深进入我的内心，哪怕我可能永远无法达到教授们的高度。我记得我曾经非常天真地问过我的教授“我要怎样才能知道所有的那些知识？”他的回答我直到今天都记忆犹新：“你没有必要知道所有的知识，但是你需要知道去哪里寻找。”

当然，寻找信息和知识不仅需要有能力问对问题（什么是值得寻找的），而且要知道去哪里找寻，以及提炼的技能。我一直都推崇严格的文献学研究方法（这种方法现在在西方知道的人越来越少），这个方法也是我中国的、日本的、法国的还有美国的老师们传授给我的。

在我上述所有的经历中，我所从事的研究方向就是唐代时期的敦煌。虽然唐代充满传奇色彩，但它无疑代表了中国文化中一些最好的部分，也同时代表了中亚文化精髓。唐文化的那种精妙和复杂，事实上是一个大同世界的绝好说明，这种文化在公元620至750期间一度辉煌，之后便衰败了。但正是这种世界大同文化的复杂性吸引了我。令人感叹的是，那样的一个大同世界最初只是佛教的大同，之后反而逐渐演变成为了文化和经济运动的发源地，而这些又都超越了宗教的因素。这种包容和兼收并蓄也是佛教的一部分。佛教的精髓便是宽容，四海大同和惠及众生。如世界大同一样，佛教连同它的思想和文化被那些去中亚做生意的商人们也一同带了过去，这些商人们对于佛家在资金上的慷慨资助也使得佛教得以广泛传播。

无需多言，唐代初期的中国无比自信和充满底气，它不仅对外国人毫不恐惧，反而欢迎外国人在政府中担任高官。唐代的文化变成了东亚的大同文化（就艺术和建筑而言，有的时候被称作“唐代的国际美学”），思想自由交流汇聚，这大概是关于软实力描述的最好的例子，没有比这更好的了。

这种强有力的文化和思想的融合造就了当时耀眼的艺术和建筑，所有这些都包含在敦煌丰富的素材中，这也是我为之着迷的地方。四海大同的精神在敦煌遗迹考古和后续研究中也体现得淋漓尽致。其中最有名的是王道士的故事，以及一些其他西方和日本的学者和考古工作者，如斯坦因(AurelStein)、伯希和(PaulPelliot)、奥登堡(SergeiOldenburg)还有大谷光瑞等，他们从藏经洞搬出了文献和艺术品，让这伟大的遗迹和发现呈现在世人面前。虽然这么做并不是全无问题，但是这些考古学家将这些丝绸之路的发现带到伦敦、巴黎、圣彼得堡、东京还有其他很多城市，从而建立了中国以外敦煌学研究的基础。也是因为如此，敦煌的大同精神和它的荣耀得以在中国以外的地区传播。后来在中国，敦煌学的研究也逐渐繁荣起来；在过去的30年里，东西方的世界在学术的交流中得以重新联系在一起。

作为学生和教授来研究敦煌，我很享受敦煌学给我带来的额外的“大同”----与来自北美、欧洲、日本和中国的学者们所建立起的学者的国际群体。

1987年，当我第一次参加在香港举行的敦煌会议的时候，我着实被到会的学者们震撼到了，他们来自法国、美国、苏联、日本和中国，他们秉持着国际合作的精神，聚集到香港讨论他们所热爱的敦煌以及敦煌独特的资源。我在研究生阶段和后来敦煌研究的学术生涯由此变成了无数空中飞行的轨迹，我得以有机会在台北、巴黎、东京和伦敦学习和研究。我研究的高峰，毋庸置疑，是我在中国的时候，同其他敦煌研究的学者们分别跟随密西根大学丝绸之路基金会和耶鲁大学来到甘肃，受到敦煌研究院和院长樊锦诗的热情接待。这对于西方的学者而言是非常宝贵的机会，和知名的敦煌学专家，比如彭金章、王惠民、马德、荣新江，还有很多其他人一起探讨。荣新江的“敦煌学十八讲”不仅对敦煌学研究的概况做了最全面的概述，更涵盖了敦煌的方方面面。从这些还有无数其他的学者那里学习，使我可以更好地深入研究敦煌和中国中世纪时期的生活风貌，并不断探索这个伟大的领域。

我期待着可以在今后的文章中分享这一领域的迷人发现，并能够更细致地描绘敦煌中蕴藏的丰富文化。这些文化应该被世界上更多的人所知晓。

译者简介：

卞京丽，北京工商大学英语学士，英国诺丁汉大学教育学硕士，北京大学心理学学士。曾任职于清华大学负责海外学术项目，之后加入科威特石油公司，现为DKT国际北京工作。

以下为原文：

Dunhuang Studies aren’t well-known in the West. When friends and family ask what I research, I mention a unique archaeological site on the Silk Road in the far west of China in Gansu. Few know “Gansu,” or “Dunhuang” or certainly “Mogao.” But in the unfurrowed ground of ignorance lies the wealth of potential harvests. The mention of Dunhuang, Mogao, and Gansu all open doors for fascinating stories of world history, China, cosmopolitanism, and, if I can immodestly say, the story of my own formation as a scholar and indeed my life.

Yet, how do we ever begin to articulate our life paths, how we got from A to B and where we might be (or expect to be!) in 20 or 30 years? I had a strange sense of what I wanted to unfold in my life even as a high school student: I wanted to be an academic. How I went from that strange assumption at the age of 14 to spending my life researching a remote location in the far west of China is due to a confluence (缘) of factors. In the 1980’s when I entered university I was determined to do something challenging, to come out with a skill and something tangible. But to make it even more challenging, I wanted to start from zero and study a topic I knew nothing about. How better than to study Chinese language and literature. China was a terra incognita for most Americans in the 1980’s, having only established relations in the early 1970’s and opening up for limited travel a few years later. But knowledge about China was scare in that time. Remember that the ease of the internet was still more than a decade away and that all information had to be gleaned from the occasional magazine and books, most local libraries having only a scant amount of volumes on China in that day and age. My boarding school, though top-notch, offered little exposure to Chinese culture and certainly none to language. Indeed, when I returned there for a visit in 1989 after having graduated from university with a degree in Chinese language, the administration was so proud to introduce me to the first-ever Chinese national to attend. (I’m happy to say that his was a success story as he went on to found eLong a decade later.) But now that high school offers an A.P. course in Chinese Language and Culture and the possibility a year-long study abroad in China, utterly unthinkable in my days.

But it wasn’t just that China with its language and culture was a terra incognita and an extremely tough challenge. I certainly knew enough as a 17 year-old high school student to be fascinated by its standing in the world cultures. Of the so-called four “great civilizations” of the Bronze Age (Egypt, Mesopotamia, the Indus Valley, and China) only China, by far, retained a remarkable continuity that all others had lost; compare Chinese characters to Hieroglyphs, cuneiform, the as-of-yet undeciphered Harrappan script. To be able to access that past and present simultaneously through Chinese characters never mind the ongoing culture seemed too a unique opportunity for a curious mind to pass up.

So off to university I went to study a language and culture out of vogue (it was all about Japan in those days...) to start from zero knowledge in a formidable challenge. My Chinese course was 8AM every morning and run by a strict Chinese woman who knew the role of shame in learning. (In addition to her clever use of shame in getting us to learn, my most potent memory the pain in my hand as we wrote and rewrote Chinese characters thousands of times every week.) The turning point in learning came when I went to Taiwan my sophomore year, eager to enter China and begin learning the culture for real. I went back to Taiwan after I graduated, when I won a scholarship to study as a graduate student in the Department of Literature 中文系 at Zhengzhi University政治大學. It was there that I was formally introduced to Dunhuang and its manuscripts, fortunate to spend time with to formidable professors Li Fengmao李豐楙and Luo Zongtao羅宗濤. And it was through them that I learned of the scholarship of Victor Mair 梅維恆 of the University of Pennsylvania on Dunhuang mansucripts and literature. I started the PhD program at Penn and then went off to Paris at the Ecole pratiques des Hautes Etudes to complete an MPhil working with the scholars Michel Soymié and Jean-Pierre Drège. The next step was study in Japan at Waseda University早稲田大学 with the Buddhologist Fukui Fumimasa 福井文雅. I ended up back at Penn to finish my dissertation with Victor Mair of Penn and Stephen Teiser at Princeton.

![](https://t2.qpic.cn/mblogpic/fe28b2f4ca564fe58ce8/2000)

This list may seem like a lot of names but they deserve to be mentioned not simply because of the sheer time they kindly spent with me as a graduate student but rather for their dedication to their craft as scholars and their expertise in their fields. (Alas, researchers often work quietly in the background obscured from the general public!) All these professors tried to impart knowledge, but I was inept and slow. I think more than the bits of knowledge that I wish I’d retained now in retrospect, it was a recognition of the passion and rigorous scholarly standards that I took to heart even if I might not achieve them. I remember naively saying to a professor after his most erudite lecture, “How will I ever know all of that.” His response has always stayed with me, “You don’t necessarily need to know it all but you do need to know where to find it.” Of course finding information and knowledge requires not only being able to ask the right question (what is worth finding), but also the knowledge of where, and the skills at extraction. I will always prize the rigorous philological approach (an approach I fear might be in the process of being lost) my teachers from China, Japan, France and the United States tried to impart to me.

So my subject of study throughout these adventures was Dunhuang in the Tang Dynasty. Although the Tang period is romanticized, it nonetheless represents some of the best aspects not only of Chinese but also of Central Asian culture in terms of its sophistication. Its sophistication and complexity are in fact a wonderful example of cosmopolitanism, especially from 620-750 after which the cosmopolitanism ebbed, and indeed this complexity is what drew me to it. What is remarkable is that cosmopolitanism was initially Buddhist cosmopolitanism. China in this sense became in turn the home of cultural cosmopolitan movements in culture and economy transcending religious factions. But that inclusiveness is part of Buddhism as a religion and its flexibility. Buddhism is quintessentially tolerant, cosmopolitan and portable. Like so much of cosmopolitanism however, Buddhism together with its ideas and culture was furthered by businessmen, merchants who traveled from Central Asia and whose financial support of Buddhism greatly helped it spread. Needless to say, China in the early Tang was receptive buoyed by a confidence and assurance that, far from xenophobic, even embraced foreigners serving in the upper echelons of the government. The culture of the Tang in turn became the cosmopolitan culture (sometimes called “Tang International Style” in terms of art and architecture) of East Asia where ideas spread freely, and here we have no better example soft power at its best.

So it was this powerful mix of culture and ideas formulated into stunning art and architecture embraced by the wealth of materials at Dunhuang that I found so attractive. But that spirit of cosmopolitanism finds a fascinating parallel in the site’s rediscovery and consequent research. The well-known story of Abbot Wang 王道士and the role of Western and Japanese scholar-explorers such as Aurel Stein斯坦因, Paul Pelliot伯希和, Sergei Oldenburg, and Count Otani大谷 光瑞, who brought the site to the attention of the modern world, by carrying off much of the textual and portable art from the library cave is now part of history, though not unproblematically so. In doing so, these explorers brought these Silk Road finds to London, Paris, St. Petersburg, and Tokyo, among other cities, and thereby established the foundations for Dunhuang Studies outside of China where they began to blossom. Hence, the cosmopolitanism of Dunhuang and its glory was re-spread to world outside of China. Later in China, Dunhuang Studies also flourished, and in the last 30 years these worlds begin to reconnect in scholarly exchanges.

Researching Dunhuang as a graduate student and professor, I enjoyed an additional form of cosmopolitanism it offers – a rich international community of scholars from North America to Europe to Japan and China. Attending my first Dunhuang conference in Hong Kong in 1987, I was struck by the range of scholars from France, America, the Soviet Union, Japan, and China, who, in their spirit of international cooperation, had gathered together in Hong Kong to discuss their passion for Dunhuang and its unique resources. My graduate and academic career investigating Dunhuang thus became a far-ranging trajectory that was an opportunity to spend time in Taibei, Paris, Tokyo, and London. The high-point of my studies was, needles to say, time spent in China, specifically working with a collection of scholars in trips organized by the Silk Road Foundation, University of Michigan, and later by Yale University all kindly hosted by the Dunhuang Research Academy 敦煌研究院 through the generosity of the director Fan Jinshi樊錦詩院長. These were unique opportunities for western scholars to spend time with renowned Dunhuang specialists such as (and among many) Peng Jinzhang 彭金章, Wang Huimin 王惠民, Ma De 馬德, and Rong Xinjiang 荣新江.I might add that the latter’s work “敦煌学十八讲” provides an excellent overview not only of Dunhuang Studies but of all things Dunhuang. whose work I might add is the best comprehensive overview of Dunhuang and Dunhuang Studies. Learning from these and so many other scholars permitted me to delve into Dunhuang and medieval life in China and explore this remarkable field. I look forward to sharing some of these fascinating explorations in future articles to describe in detail the rich culture of Dunhuang that strongly deserves to be better known by the world over.
