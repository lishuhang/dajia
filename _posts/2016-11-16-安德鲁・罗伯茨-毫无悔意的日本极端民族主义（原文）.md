My wife Susan and I have just returned from a holiday in Japan, a country we had never visited before. We were impressed with the country; the friendliness of the people, the beauty of the shrines, the cuisine, and the richness of the culture. But as an historian I was utterly shocked and outraged by the way their museums depict the Showa period of 1931-45, which was an absolute disgrace. In my professional opinion, their attitude towards China in particular, but also to my own country, Great Britain, and to the United States for the 1941-45 period, constitutes nothing short of a national libel.

*![](https://img1.gtimg.com/cul/pics/hv1/73/209/2155/140182243.jpg)*

Of course every country remembers the events of that terrible time in world history differently: in Germany the history of the Nazis is dealt with sensitively and responsibly, as befits a model modern country that is conscious of having unleashed terrible suffering in the past under their dictator Adolf Hitler. In France, the period of the Nazi Occupation from 1940-44 is still a subject for painful reflection and much internal debate about the terrible choices ordinary French people had to make. In Britain and America the War is celebrated as a victory over evil, which it undoubtedly was, without much moral complications beyond the carpet-bombing of Germany and Japan and the dropping of the two Atomic bombs on Japan, which were both defensible under the circumstances of Total War. In Russia the 27 million who died are solemnly and powerfully recalled in memorial after memorial.

In Japan, however, there is an almost complete refusal to accept any responsibility whatever for what happened during the Showa period, and again and again I found the use of devious phraseology to imply that other countries �C including China, Britain and America �C were actually responsible for what happened, rather than the Tokyo government of the Emperor Hirohito. A country cannot move forward unless it is able to see clearly what happened to it in the past, and on that criterion Japan �C for all its attractive features and commendable work ethic and so on �C is clearly refusing to face up to its heavy historical responsibilities.

At the Metropolitan-Edo Museum in Tokyo, which is otherwise a fine place of learning covering the history of Tokyo over many centuries, there is a gap in the story between the between the beginning of the 1930s and the National General Mobilization Order of 1938, and no mention of the Japanese aggression against China since 1931. It speaks of the ‘oppression of the people’, but only in reference to the Japanese people, not the Chinese, as though the Japanese people had not wholeheartedly supported the war. It also takes refuge in completely meaningless sentences such as ‘Meanwhile, the township and neighbourhood associates that were organized as the most local unit of wartime administration reinforced collective responsibility and maintained monitoring to carry out the war plan.’   Whatever that means, it is not helpful for a better understanding of the period.

*![](https://img1.gtimg.com/cul/pics/hv1/74/209/2155/140182244.jpg)*

There is no mention of Marco Polo Bridge, or the invasion of Manchuria, or the Rape of Nanjing, or the treatment of Chinese prisoners of war, or anything at all to put the exhibits into their proper historical context. ‘On April 18 1942, less than a year after the outbreak of the Pacific War,’ one exhibit states, ‘Tokyo experienced its first air raid by the United States military aircraft. It was a surprise attack.’ There is no mention at all of the fact that the war began because of Japan’s surprise attack on the American Fleet at Pearl Harbor in Hawaii. To a Japanese citizen visiting the exhibition, or a Martian stepping off a spacecraft, it would seem that the Americans bombed Tokyo for no good reason at all.

Yet the exhibition also states: ‘To prepare against such air raids the Air Defense Law had been issued in April 1937’, without any reference to the fact that Japan had been fighting a vicious war of unprovoked aggression for six years against the Chinese. There is a good deal about the way the American B-29 bombers �C which the museum correctly states ‘is the huge bombing aircraft which was made by the Boeing Company in America’ - were ‘reducing Tokyo to ashes’, as indeed they did, and how ‘the city was completely destroyed, and its citizens suffered from severe ravages of war,’ but no explanation as to why that was. Indeed the only reference to China is ‘In June 1944 the first fully-fledged air raid on the Japanese mainland was carried out by the B-29 bombers that had left military bases in China.’ Someone without knowledge of the period, such a Japanese schoolchild of which there were many visiting the museum, but who are not taught that period in Japan’s history honestly, would probably conclude from this that China was the aggressor, not Japan.

In Hiroshima, the city that was devastated by the nuclear bomb dropped on it by the Allies on 6 August 1945, there is a very powerful and moving museum, but there too there is no attempt to put the use of the bomb into any historical context until after visitors have left the exhibition, when on one of several displays there is a single sentence stating that Japan was responsible. Yet there too the museum attempts to skew the historical record, stating ‘The US believed that the atomic bomb could end the war, Soviet influence after the war would be restricted and domestically the tremendous cost of development would be justified.’ The idea that either of those last two reasons weighed in the minds of President Harry S. Truman and his advisors at all, compared to their desperate desire to end the Second World War as soon as possible, is a terrible libel on them, indeed a conspiracy theory without serious foundation. Issues of foreign policy towards Russia and financial justification in Congress were simply not the true reasons why Truman took the grave decision to incinerate 140,000 Japanese civilians in Hiroshima: it was to save the lives of a quarter of a million American soldiers who were preparing to invade the Japanese home islands, and to bring the long years of slaughter to an end.  It is monstrous of the modern Japanese authorities to permit such conspiracy theories to be aired in such a public place without giving any historical evidence for their baseless charges.

By far the worst example that I saw on my trip of unrepentant Japanese ultra-nationalism and refusal to accept any blame for the events of 1931-45, however, was to be found at the notorious Yasukuni Jinja (Shrine) in Tokyo, which was built ‘to comfort the souls of those who made the ultimate sacrifice for their country and to hand down their accomplishments to posterity.’ There the unprovoked aggression against China �C which led to the deaths of over 15 million Chinese people between 1931 and 1945 �C is simply called ‘The Chia Incident’, the invasion of Manchuria is described merely as ‘The Manchuria Incident’ and the history of what Japan did in Korea is summed up as ‘The Korean Problem’, as though Japan was merely solving a problem there, rather than laying waste to the country and turning much of its female population into prostitutes for their soldiers.

The attack on Pearl Harbor and its aftermath, in which Japan launched similarly unprovoked attacks on Myanmar, the Philippines (where 17% of the Filipino population were to die), Australia, New Guinea, Indo-China, Malaysia, Singapore and other peaceful countries is actually blamed on the United States and Britain. To make matters worse at the Yasukuni Shrine, among the huge amounts of military hardware �C kamikaze Ohka planes, one-man submarines, Zero fighters, and so on �C that are on display, which is perfectly acceptable in what after all in a military museum, there is a locomotive from the notorious Thai-Burma Railroad, a railway line that was built by prisoners of war in the most inhuman, atrocious conditions. Javanese, Malayan Tamils of Indian origin, Burmese, Chinese, Thai and other Southeast Asians were forced to work on the railway as well as British, American and Australian prisoners of war, and it is estimated that up to 200,000 died. Yet the Japanese display a locomotive in the central lobby of the Museum attached to the shrine.

*![](https://img1.gtimg.com/cul/pics/hv1/198/211/2155/140182878.jpg)*

I have been to the Kachanaburi War Cemetery in Thailand where many of the Western victims of the railway are buried and was profoundly moved by it. To see the locomotive proudly displayed was shocking. When one visits Auschwitz in Poland, the presentation of the Holocaust against the Jews is accompanied with manifestations of the profound regret of the modern German state; there is absolutely nothing of that nature at Yasukini. Indeed there is no indication whatever that any prisoners of war were ever mistreated by the Japanese at any time.

Similarly the fanatical kamikaze pilots who Japan deployed in a desperate attempt to turn the tide of the war in the Pacific are lauded, as though the phrase from which they took their name ‘Divine Wind’ somehow conferred genuinely spiritual authority on people who were in realty no better than the suicide-murderers who destroyed the Twin Towers in Manhattan on 9/11.

Indeed, worse than that, there is a deliberate attempt to blame China and the Western powers for what happened. The pro-Japanese puppet government of Manchukuo which was established by the Japanese invaders on 1 March 1932 is presented as though it was a genuine autonomous state. ‘A confederation of five ethnic groups established a new state in Manchuria,’ states the Yasukuni exhibition, next to a large four-coloured flag of the country. In fact it was imposed on the people by the government in Tokyo and collapsed as soon as the Japanese Army left in 1945.

The whole history of Japan is presented as a series of slights and exploitations of the innocent Japanese, none of which should be forgiven or forgotten, however small or long ago. On Sakhalin Island in 1807, for example, we are told ‘The Russians stole food and other items, and set fire to Japanese houses.’ This reaches its crescendo in a letter of 7 November 1937 written by  a Japanese major in the 7th Infantry Regiment who writes that ‘Contempt by Chinese common people as well as Chinese forces for the Japanese has become extremely intense, which makes me cry with anger and sadness not only as an officer but also as a citizen. … I also heard that Japanese women were raped by Chinese men.’

Since the infamous Rape of Nanjing began on 13 December 1937, only five weeks later, which was to go on for six weeks and cost the lives of anything up to 300,000 innocent Chinese civilians, the choice of this letter by the Japanese museum authorities amounts to nothing less than an attempt to blame the Rape on the Chinese rather than the Japanese. As an historian who had written some 19 books over the past thirty years, including several on the Second World War period, I was utterly shocked by this monstrous perversion of the truth.

There are other references to the ‘revival of Chinese terrorism’ and the Second Shanghai Incident ‘which is again triggered by the Chinese side’ and the only actual mention of what is called ‘The Nanjing Incident’ is summed up by the statement that ‘General Matsui told his men that they were to maintain strict military discipline and that anyone committing unlawful acts would be severely punished … The Chinese soldiers disguised in civilian clothes were severely prosecuted.’ The truth about the six weeks of rape and murder by the Japanese army going from door to door raping every woman and girl in the city is completely ignored. Of the Wuhan Campaign, the exhibit states ‘A perfect care was taken to secure the safety of residents and historical and cultural monuments.’

The Japanese surprise attack on Pearl Harbor, which took place without a prior declaration of war on 7 December 1941, is entirely blamed on President Franklin Roosevelt’s oil embargo, ‘which triggered the war.’ The idea that Japan might have done what was necessary for the embargo to be lifted �C that is, evacuate French Indo-China, which they had invaded �C does not seem to have occurred to the curators of the museum. There is even an exhibit entitled ‘Japan’s Quest for Avoiding a War’, an announcement of 6 November 1943 which states that ‘The countries of Greater East Asia will cultivate friendly relations with all the countries if the world and work for the abolition of racial discrimination, the promotion of cultural intercourse and the opening of resources throughout the world and contribute thereby to the progress of mankind.’ There is no indication that the truth about what Japan cynically called The Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere was completely different from these high-sounding ideals.

*![](https://img1.gtimg.com/cul/pics/hv1/75/209/2155/140182245.jpg)*

It is said that when a Japanese schoolchild was asked by his teacher ‘Why did the Japanese attack Pearl Harbor?’ he replied ‘In retaliation for the American devastation of Hiroshima.’ That might be a joke, but it would not surprise me if the story is true, so skewed and unhistorical is the telling of that period of Japanese history in Japan’s museums and shrines. A great nation should not feel the need to lie about their past, and should have greater sensitivity for the deaths of the 15 million Chinese people that they caused, as well as the sufferings of many other nations, including my own. This officially-sanctioned combination of ultra-nationalism, self-pity, sly innuendo (as over the Chinese men raping Japanese women rather than Japanese men raping Chinese women) and sheer falseness makes for bad foreign policy, and terrible history.
