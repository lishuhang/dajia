Leonardo da Vinci's Salvator Mundi, the world's most expensive painting, is about to go on display in the stunning new Louvre Abu Dhabi. The Italian master's haunting image of Christ will hang in a gallery resembling a compact Arabian medina, under a vast dome inspired by overlapping palm fronds, with a cool oasis lapping nearby. As the showpiece of an Islamic state, the setting is deliciously incongruous. “What would a Muslim want with a portrait of Christ?” puzzled Newsweek magazine in a headline.

*![Salvator Mundi](https://img1.gtimg.com/orignal/pics/hv1/100/234/2280/148316770.jpg)

Salvator Mundi*

It's also deeply fitting �C and not only because it returns Jesus, still draped in the borrowed garb of Renaissance Italy, to his Middle Eastern homeland. Despite heavy restoration and lingering doubts about its authenticity, the painting sold at Christie's last November for a record-shattering $450 million, an increase of 750,000,000% over its sale price less than sixty years ago. That astounding revaluation reflects what may be the most remarkable journey of any masterpiece �C a journey that in several ways has now come full circle.

The story begins in 1500, a year of prophesies that many Christians believed would usher in the End Times of the world. One reason for the apocalyptic doom and gloom was that Europe was enmeshed in a series of struggles for power and land, known as the Great Italian Wars, that would stretch on for sixty-five years. As the new century dawned, the French king Louis XII had the upper hand. Only two years into his reign, Louis was riding high in the saddle after capturing the Duchy of Milan and was all set to march on the Kingdom of Naples.

Formerly a great feudal lord who had opposed the French monarchy in the so-called Mad War, Louis had married his disabled and sterile cousin Joan on the orders of the previous king, who had hoped a barren marriage would extinguish the rival line. When he unexpectedly ascended to the throne, Louis had the pope annul the marriage and instead married Anne of Brittany, his predecessor's widow. Now ruler of Milan and soon to be king of Naples as well as of France, he needed a dramatic way to emblazon the God-given authority of his new dynasty.

For all his talents and growing fame, Leonardo da Vinci was not the most obvious candidate. For seventeen years he had enjoyed the patronage of Ludovic Sforza, the defeated duke of Milan. When Louis's troops had invaded the city, his archers had lined up within view of a larger-than-life clay model that Leonardo had built for an equestrian monument and used it for target practice. The artist had fled to Venice, where he found work as a military architect and engineer, before retreating to a monastery in Florence. Yet the French king's commission of a portrait of Christ from the most celebrated artist of the age was not something to refuse lightly.

If he had seen the studies that Leonardo started sketching for the composition, Louis might well have detected a mystical Christ raising his fingers in benediction on the new French order, holding a crystal globe that represents the conjunction of heavenly and worldly powers, just as they were joined in the person of the king. Leonardo would have been well aware that Louis was making a declaration of power as well as an act of piety and patronage: specifically, French power over Italy and even Europe. And yet, as with all great works of art, the portrait would take on a life of its own that would mock as well as flatter its royal viewer.

Leonardo, the Renaissance's greatest polymath, had many other interests besides painting, and in 1502 he entered the service of Cesare Borgia, the son of Pope Alexander VI, acting as his military architect and engineer and travelling with him throughout Italy. It was several more years before the portrait of Christ as savior of the world was finished, and by then Louis had already ignominiously lost Naples. When he received the painting, Christ's enigmatic gaze may have reminded him that eternal power was beyond the reach of any mortal, however great.

In 1515 Louis died without a male heir and was succeeded by his cousin Francis, who recaptured Milan the same year. The following year Leonardo moved to France. Salvator Mundi had already cemented his reputation there, but he brought with him more samples of his wares, including an enigmatic portrait that would become known as the Mona Lisa. Francis installed him in a turreted manor house connected by a tunnel to his royal residence, and the French even tried to remove the Last Supper from Milan, along with the wall it was painted on. When Leonardo died three years later, Francis cradled his head in his arms. “There had never been another man born in the world,” he lamented, “who knew as much as Leonardo.” Through its association with da Vinci, France had staked its claim to take over from Italy as the new center of the Renaissance.

*![](https://img1.gtimg.com/orignal/pics/hv1/84/234/2280/148316754.jpg)*

The painting remained in the Louvre Palace for more than a century until, in 1625, Henrietta Maria of France married Charles I of England. With her she brought the Salvator Mundi, which hung in pride of place in her palace at Greenwich. There it joined the masterpieces by Titian, Rubens, Raphael, Caravaggio and Rembrandt that Charles, the greatest picture collector of the age, purchased across the continent, including the entire collection of the Duke of Mantua.

*![Henrietta Maria](https://img1.gtimg.com/orignal/pics/hv1/80/234/2280/148316750.jpg)

Henrietta Maria*

Charles funded his expensive hobby and lavish lifestyle by levying unpopular taxes without parliamentary consent. He quarreled with the English parliament, believing that the divine right of kings invested in him unlimited powers. His marriage to a Catholic and failure to support Protestants drew the enmity of reformed groups and precipitated the Civil War. As the royal troops fought Oliver Cromwell's parliamentary armies, Henrietta Maria fled for France, leaving the Salvator Mundi behind.

Charles was tried, convicted and, in January 1649, executed for high treason, and a giant “Commonwealth Sale” was held to pay the king's creditors and further the “publick uses of this Commonwealth.” Most of the royal picture collection was sold at knockdown prices, and Salvator Mundi went to John Stone, a mason.  If he appreciated just what was hanging on his wall, Stone did not get to enjoy it for many years. When the monarchy was restored in 1660 under Charles II, an Act of Parliament recovered much of the dispersed Crown property, including Salvator Mundi, which Stone was obliged to return.

The painting's fortunes then entered a long downward spiral. When Charles's younger brother ascended the throne as James II, he gave the masterpiece to his mistress, Catherine Sedley. The new king's Catholicism drew opposition from England's Protestant elite, who launched the Glorious Revolution and exiled James to France, where he lived as a pretender with a court sponsored by Louis XIV. Without royal protection, James's mistress lost her position in society. Salvator Mundi was handed down through her family, increasingly worn, crudely overpainted several times, and eventually so blackened that it was barely given a second glance. In 1900, with its provenance completely forgotten, it was sold for a small sum as a minor work by a follower of Leonardo. In 1958 it emerged again at auction, netting 45 UK Pounds, only to disappear again for nearly fifty years.

The modern history of Leonardo's lost masterpiece begins in 2005, with its rediscovery in a regional estate auction in the U.S. The buyers, a consortium that included New York art dealer Alexander Parish, were taking a gamble: they paid only $10,000, but were prepared to spend vastly more to prove the painting's authenticity. Amid widespread skepticism David Ekserdjian, a bow-tied British art historian, was among the first to detect the master's hand. Experts in forensics examined the painting, and Dianne Dwyer Modestini, a conservator at New York University, was hired to restore it. The worst damage stemmed from a knot in the walnut panel, which had split early on; otherwise, after cleaning, the hands, curls, orb, and much of the drapery were revealed to be largely intact, while the parts of the face that remained had retained all their layers. X-rays uncovered pentimenti �C preliminary ideas that the artist later discarded �C including the right thumb in a different position, suggesting that the painting was unlikely to be the work of a copyist. Infrared imaging also revealed touches characteristic of Leonardo's later paintings, such as the Mona Lisa: executing the head with the aid of a cartoon, and smoothing the paint with the heel of the hand to create soft effects of shadow and light, a technique known as sfumato.

Further research uncovered two cartoons at Windsor Castle that were clearly preparatory studies of drapery for Christ's gown, and a 1650 etching that matched the composition. As the clues fell into place, the owners showed the painting to a group of international Leonardo experts, and then to conservators at the Metropolitan Museum and London's National Gallery. The latter was planning its own major Leonardo exhibition for 2011, and late in the day, it controversially decided to make Salvator Mundi the centerpiece, giving it the final stamp of authenticity as one of fifteen or sixteen Leonardo paintings in existence. So at least said its backers: a minority of Leonardo experts remained unconvinced, suggesting that the portrait was at best only partly by Leonardo's hand.

When the exhibition closed, Swiss art dealer Yves Bouvier bought the work at Sotheby's for $80 million. Bouvier had the perfect client in mind: the Russian oligarch Dmitry Rybolovlev. In Soviet times a cardiologist, Rybolovlev had made billions in the new Russian economy from potash, used as fertilizer. His rise was accompanied by ecological disasters and an arrest on murder charges (he was later acquitted), and in classic oligarch style he moved to Monaco, where in 2011 he became president of the football club AS Monaco. Rybolovlev also bought Donald Trump's Florida seaside mansion for $95 million, and has moved in some of the same circles as Trump. In the 2016 Football Leaks scandal it was alleged that he had illegally bought players' shares and manipulated their prices; the Panama Papers scandal also led to media speculation that he had set up offshore companies to conceal art purchases from his wife Elena, who he had met at medical school and from whom, at the time of the sale, he was in the middle of a messy divorce.

Like medieval monarchs and Renaissance bankers, modern plutocrats and oligarchs are famous for burnishing their cultural credentials by splurging vast sums on trophy art. Two days after the sale, Bouvier sent Salvator Mundi to the fertilizer king's Manhattan penthouse with a new price tag of $125 million. As the only private individual in the world to own a Leonardo painting, Rybolovlev catapulted to the apex of the world's art-collecting elite. Yet before long he and Bouvier took up cudgels in a bitter and very public legal dispute that remains unresolved. Rybolovlev accused his dealer of swindling him by marking up the price of the painting by $45 million; when Bouvier tried to enter one of Rybolovlev's lavish properties, he was arrested.

The story of Salvator Mundi became yet another cautionary tale about the absurdities of the high-end art world, where phenomenal wealth is ginned up through sheer force of marketing for a painting of disputed merit. So it might have remained if Rybolovlev himself had not, a mere four years after he bought the portrait, consigned it for sale at Christie's. The estimate was $100 million, less than the price paid for a Jean-Michel Basquiat; but if the Russian was expecting that it would fail to reach its reserve, thereby aiding his case against Bouvier, he was to be disappointed. The room was packed with some of the world's wealthiest people: among them Italian businessman Giancarlo Giammetti and his partner, the fashion designer Valentino; American businessman, politician and philanthropist Michael Bloomberg; and Chinese billionaire investor and art collector Liu Yiqian who, journalists excitedly noted, started life as a taxi driver. When the price reached $370 million, a phone buyer raised the stakes by bidding an even $400 million.

After weeks of speculation, the buyer was revealed as Mohammad bin Salman, the Saudi crown prince who is trying to drag his nation into the 19th or possibly 20th century, in part by means of an austerity drive. Buying a painting of Christ, in defiance of the widespread Muslim proscription against figurative images, seemed calculated to provoke already irate clerics.

*![Mohammad bin Salman, the Saudi crown prince](https://img1.gtimg.com/orignal/pics/hv1/94/234/2280/148316764.jpg)

Mohammad bin Salman, the Saudi crown prince*

The Saudis quickly announced that the painting would hang in the new Louvre Abu Dhabi, which will thus outdo the institution that currently holds the record for the most paid for a painting: the national gallery of Qatar, which happens to be locked in a bitter feud with Saudi Arabia. Louis XII might have approved.

*![](https://img1.gtimg.com/orignal/pics/hv1/99/234/2280/148316769.jpg)*

Salvator Mundi now sits at the heart of a grand cultural project with a $2 billion price tag, including $525 million to borrow the Louvre brand for thirty years. Sunbeams perforate the giant dome like stars in the desert sky, dappling the chalk-white micro-city sheltering beneath. Built out at sea, the museum both belongs to the city and exists beyond it, just as great art both inheres to a place and is universal. Leonardo’s savior will gaze at the international crowds that will come to see him, his otherworldly expression as enigmatic as ever, conveying perhaps benediction, perhaps admonition. Either way, his resting place is a perfect home for a work that was always as much about politics, power and prestige as it was about religion or art.
